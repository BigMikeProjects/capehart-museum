The Story

Don & Rita Capehart:
Accidental Archivists

For over twenty years, Don Capehart worked for Western Electric, the manufacturing arm of the Bell System. He watched as technology evolved, as electromechanical switches gave way to digital systems, as an era quietly ended.

Then came 1984. The court-ordered breakup of AT&T scattered a century of corporate history to the winds. Equipment that had connected millions of Americans was suddenly deemed obsolete, destined for the scrap heap.

"When Western Electric ceased operations, they were just throwing this stuff away. History—real, tangible history—going into dumpsters."

Don Capehart

Don and Rita couldn't watch it happen. What started as rescuing a few pieces grew into an obsession, then a mission. They filled their home. They bought a warehouse—a former Coca-Cola bottling plant spanning 15,000 square feet. They kept saving.

By the time they were done, they had assembled what multiple publications called the largest private telephone museum in the world: over 8,000 artifacts spanning from the 1870s to the digital age.

The Context

Ma Bell: The Company
That Connected America

1876–1920

The Invention Era

Alexander Graham Bell patents the telephone. Western Electric becomes the manufacturing arm of the Bell System. Candlestick phones and wooden wall units bring voice communication into American homes.

1920–1950

The Operator Era

The "Hello Girls"—over 350,000 women—become the human network connecting America. Manual switchboards require skill, patience, and an unfailing pleasant demeanor. The telephone operator becomes a cultural icon.

1950–1984

The Automation Era

Strowger switches and crossbar systems automate call routing. Color phones transform telephones from utility to fashion. The Bell System reaches its peak as a regulated monopoly serving virtually every American.

January 1, 1984

The Breakup

A court-ordered divestiture shatters AT&T into seven Regional Bell Operating Companies. Western Electric is dissolved. A century of unified telecommunications history is scattered—and much of it is discarded as "obsolete."

1984–2021

The Rescue

Don and Rita Capehart begin their decades-long mission to preserve what others are throwing away. Their collection grows to 8,000 pieces in a 15,000 square foot former bottling plant.

The Collection

Virtual Galleries

Explore four thematic collections that tell the story of how America learned to talk across distances.

Gallery A

"Number Please"

The Human Operators

Before automation, every telephone call passed through human hands. At the Bell System's peak, over 350,000 women—the "Hello Girls"—worked the switchboards, connecting callers with skill and courtesy that no machine could match.

Gallery B

"The Machine Takes Over"

Automation

Legend has it that Almon Strowger, a Kansas City undertaker, invented automatic switching because he suspected telephone operators were routing his business calls to competitors. True or not, his 1891 invention changed everything—and the clicking, stepping switches he created remained in service for over a century.

Gallery C

"Reach Out and Touch Someone"

The Consumer Era

In the late 1950s, AT&T discovered something revolutionary: people would pay more for colorful phones. The instrument that had been a utilitarian black box became a fashion statement. Princess phones. Trimlines. Payphones on every corner. Telephone booths as urban landmarks.

Gallery D

"Behind the Dial"

The Hidden Infrastructure

Every dial tone, every clear connection, every "number please"—all depended on vast invisible systems of engineering. The telephone network was the most complex machine humanity had ever built, and it worked so well that most people never thought about it.

The Ending

Closing Time: 2021

After decades of preserving history, Don and Rita Capehart made a difficult decision. It was time to downsize, time to let go.

In 2021, the collection went to auction. Pieces that had been rescued from dumpsters in 1984 scattered once again—this time to collectors, museums, and enthusiasts who recognized their value.

The building in Corsicana is quiet now. The switchboards will never light up again. The rotary dials will never click through their circuits.

Museums don't just preserve objects. They preserve the memory that objects once mattered—that someone cared enough to save them when everyone else had moved on.

What the Capeharts built was never just a collection of old telephones. It was a testament to the human need to connect, to remember, to hold onto the tangible evidence of how we once lived.

The line goes silent. But the story remains.

Remember the Landline Era?

Many of us grew up with rotary dials, busy signals, and party lines. These technologies shaped how families communicated, how teenagers found privacy, how businesses operated. The sounds and rituals of landline telephony are fading from living memory.

Take a moment to remember: the satisfying click of a rotary dial returning home. The weight of a Bakelite handset. The operator who knew your name in a small town.
